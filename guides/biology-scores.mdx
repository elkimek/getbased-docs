Biology Scores turn your lab markers into readable system-level patterns. Instead of making you inspect one biomarker at a time, getbased groups related evidence into domains such as metabolic health, cardiovascular risk, inflammation, methylation, kidney and hydration, liver and bile flow, immune balance, iron, hormones, recovery, stress resilience, cellular energy, gut-immune signaling, nerve-muscle context, and lipid membrane quality.

The numbers are score summaries, not diagnoses. They are calculated from your markers, ranges, draw dates, profile context, and supported specialty tests. AI can explain a score in plain language, but it does not invent the score or change the marker math.

<Warning>
Biology Scores are educational decision support. They can help you spot patterns, missing evidence, stale panels, and useful follow-up tests, but they are not medical diagnosis or treatment advice.
</Warning>

## Open Biology Scores

Go to **Biology Scores** from the sidebar or mobile menu.

When enough data exists, the page shows:

- **Biological Coherence** — one system-level overview across active core domains
- **score cards** — individual domains with score, coverage, confidence, evidence, and marker details
- **Coverage Planner** — the missing markers that would improve baseline coverage or optional depth
- **context check** — profile and timing context used before scores unlock

The dashboard also includes a **Biological Coherence** widget by default once Biology Scores are available. You can add individual score widgets from **Add widget**.

## Why the context check exists

Biology is not context-free. Some marker patterns should be interpreted differently depending on sex, age, cycle phase, menopause state, hormone therapy, recent hard training, acute illness, low muscle mass, light exposure, wearables, and genetics.

Before scores unlock, click **Unlock Biology Scores** / **Context check**. getbased reviews the context that could affect scoring and then unlocks all timeframe tabs for that profile.

The context check can suggest structured flags, but you stay in control. Suggestions are not applied silently.

Examples:

- Low muscle mass or neuromuscular disease can make creatinine-derived markers unreliable as filtration signals.
- Female hormone markers need cycle, menopause, or therapy context before they should be scored like ordinary cycling ranges.
- Cortisol needs sample-time context.
- Light exposure can change how vitamin D and inflammation patterns are interpreted.
- SNPs can add context to methylation, vitamin D, lipids, iron, and related domains without becoming standalone score inputs.

## Biological Coherence

**Biological Coherence** is the holistic overview. It asks: do the major systems in your blood work look internally coherent, or are several domains strained, stale, or under-tested?

It combines live core domains such as:

- metabolic health
- cardiovascular risk
- inflammation
- immune balance
- iron and blood-flow context
- methylation
- kidney and hydration
- liver and bile flow
- bone and mineral balance
- thyroid hormones
- hormone axis, when relevant
- recovery capacity, when relevant

Extended-only or specialty-heavy domains — currently Lipid Membrane, Cellular Energy, Stress Resilience, Gut–Immune Signal, and Nerve–Muscle Signal — can improve interpretation, but they should not punish you for not ordering advanced tests. Missing advanced depth lowers confidence or optional depth, not the baseline score itself.

## What each score shows

Each score section separates three different ideas:

| Field | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Pattern** | How the available marker pattern looks on a 0–100 scale |
| **Coverage** | How much of the score's useful marker set is available |
| **Confidence** | Whether the score is robust enough to trust or should be treated carefully |
| **Evidence** | Whether the score is production-backed, profile-aware, contextual, or experimental |
| **Recency / retest state** | Whether inputs are stale or drawn too far apart to combine |

A low score is not the same thing as missing data. Missing data affects coverage and confidence. A strained pattern means the markers you do have are pointing in a difficult direction.

## Marker rows and missing markers

Open a score to see:

- the plain-English question the score is checking
- core markers used for baseline signal
- markers that add better confidence
- available markers and their fit
- missing markers and whether they are core, optional, derived, or context-dependent

getbased uses lab-orderable marker names in the UI. For example, it should say **Homocysteine**, **Triglycerides**, **AST**, **ALP**, **CRP**, or **hs-CRP** — not confusing aliases like “homocysteine load” or “liver muscle signal.”

<Note>
**CRP and hs-CRP are not the same marker.** hs-CRP is used for low-grade inflammatory and cardiovascular-risk context. Standard CRP is broader inflammation context. getbased keeps them separate instead of treating them as interchangeable rows.
</Note>

## Calculated ratios

Some score inputs are calculated from ordinary labs instead of ordered directly. Examples include:

- HOMA-IR from glucose + insulin
- TG/HDL ratio from triglycerides + HDL
- Total cholesterol/HDL ratio from total cholesterol + HDL
- ApoB/ApoA-I ratio from ApoB + ApoA-I
- BUN/creatinine ratio from urea/BUN + creatinine
- NLR from neutrophils + lymphocytes

If you have the component markers, getbased can derive the ratio. If the lab provides the ratio directly, getbased can use that too.

## Specialty tests and adapters

Biology Scores can use supported specialty-test adapters when those markers are relevant:

- fatty acid panels can feed **Lipid Membrane** through omega-3 index, EPA, DHA, AA/EPA, and omega-6/3 context
- OAT / Metabolomix-style markers can feed **Cellular Energy** and **Gut–Immune** domains
- BioStarks markers can feed standard and specialty domains where the marker is mapped safely
- active B12 can satisfy B12 context in methylation when total B12 is absent

Specialty tests should improve depth, confidence, or advanced domains. You should not see a worse baseline Biological Coherence score just because you have not ordered advanced testing.

## Coverage Planner

The **Coverage Planner** answers: what should I order next if I want better Biology Scores coverage?

It separates:

- **baseline gaps** — common markers that complete core domains
- **score-by-score gaps** — missing markers per domain
- **optional advanced depth** — specialty or deeper markers that improve confidence, not baseline validity

Use **Ask chat what to order** when you want a plain-language lab plan. It starts a new chat thread and asks the AI to prioritize baseline Biological Coherence first, avoid expensive advanced tests unless they materially improve coverage, and explain which missing markers map to which scores.

<Tip>
If cost matters, start with baseline gaps before optional advanced depth. A good basic panel should get most people to useful coverage without needing fatty-acid testing, OAT, metabolomics, or other specialty panels.
</Tip>

## Timeframes

The timeframe controls (**3M**, **6M**, **1Y**, **All**) filter the lab data used for the scores. This is useful when you want to know what the last few months look like instead of your whole history.

One context check unlocks all timeframe tabs. The score can still change by timeframe if the underlying marker set changes.

## AI explanations

Each score can show **Explain this score** / **Refresh explanation**.

The explanation is generated from the deterministic score, marker values, exact ranges, missing markers, confidence state, and context flags. It should not invent thresholds. If marker evidence changes after an explanation was generated, getbased warns you and asks you to refresh it.

AI explanations are stored with the profile so they survive refresh and cross-device sync.

## How to read the output safely

Use Biology Scores to decide what to inspect, retest, or ask about next.

Good questions:

- Which domain is most strained right now?
- Is this score low because markers are actually strained, or because coverage is thin?
- Are any inputs stale or drawn too far apart?
- Which missing marker would improve the most scores?
- Is a specialty test adding real information, or is a baseline marker missing first?

Avoid reading the page as:

- a diagnosis
- a replacement for clinical review
- proof that one supplement or habit caused a change
- a reason to order every advanced marker at once

## Troubleshooting

| Symptom | What to check |
|---|---|
| Scores are locked | Run the context check first. |
| A score says “Need inputs” | Open the score or Coverage Planner to see missing core markers. |
| A calculated ratio is missing | Confirm both source markers exist in the same timeframe and are not stale. |
| A hormone score looks unavailable | Add sex, age, cycle/menopause, therapy, and draw timing context where relevant. |
| A specialty marker is visible in Labs but not in a score | The marker may not be mapped to that score, may be an optional-depth marker, or may be too stale. |
| An AI explanation shows a stale warning | Click **Refresh explanation** so it uses the current marker evidence. |
| Scores differ by timeframe | That is expected when the selected timeframe changes the available marker set. |
